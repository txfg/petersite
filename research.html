Geotechnical Research — Geosynthetic Textiles

[ feature video — frame-by-frame experiment footage ]

My work centered on the geosynthetic fabric itself — how it moves and holds water. The fellowship's bigger contribution was re-engineering how the data was captured, replacing variable, human-timed readings with continuous, instrument-grade measurement.

01 · Automated Water-Siphoning Rig

Inside a temperature- and humidity-controlled chamber, a water-filled beaker sat on one scale with a textile strip dipped in it; the fabric siphoned water into a second, empty beaker on its own scale. Rather than send someone to photograph the scales three times a day — introducing timing variability every reading — I mounted a GoPro to the chamber's glass panel to record continuously, then wrote a script that stripped the footage frame by frame into a mass-change time-series for both beakers.

"One camera turned an experiment someone had to babysit into a dataset that recorded itself."

02 · Capillary-Rise Thermal Monitoring

When the capillary-rise study passed to me, the standard method was eyeballing the highest point the water reached — far too subjective — and adding dye, which itself altered absorption at the microscopic scale and skewed the result. I requested a thermal camera and tracked the temperature differential instead, letting me watch capillary rise climb the textile vertically in real time, without touching the sample. Average reading precision improved by more than 60%.

"The dye we used to see the water was the very thing changing how it moved. Heat let me measure without interfering."
